5. How to fix this

  1. Locate the injection — search your site's footer / header / sidebar templates for the absolutely-positioned hidden link cluster (typical CSS: position:absolute;left:-9999px, opacity:0;height:0, or display:none). The DOM dumps in this report show the exact location.
  2. Audit recently modified files — look for backdoor PHP web-shells under /wp-content/, /admin/ or template directories. Disable any out-of-date CMS plugins.
  3. Rotate credentials — change all CMS administrator passwords, FTP/SSH keys. If possible, redeploy from a known-clean backup.
  4. Confirm closure — reply to team@abuseradar.org. We re-render the page within 12 hours and confirm the fix in this report.

7. Reporting the attacker domains help break the chain

Cleaning your own site stops the bleed, but the attacker keeps operating from their infrastructure — and very often that infrastructure sits behind a major provider like Cloudflare. Cloudflare itself is a legitimate, widely-used service; the issue is that bad actors abuse its anonymisation layer to obscure which hosting provider actually serves the spam content. They are not at fault, but they are uniquely positioned to act on well-documented reports — especially when the targets are government, education, or other public-interest institutions.

Filing a short, evidence-backed abuse report is one of the most effective steps you (or any technical contact at the affected institution) can take. It helps the provider identify recurring offenders and, in repeat cases, suspend the account hosting the spam network. The links below all accept reports without an account; the bundle ID and the URLs in this report are sufficient as evidence.

  • Cloudflare — preferred form: abuse.cloudflare.com. Email fallback: abuse@cloudflare.com (registrar-side: registrar-abuse@cloudflare.com). Especially impactful for government / .edu targets.
  • Google Safe Browsing — safebrowsing.google.com/report_phish · flags the attacker domains in Chrome / Search.
  • Google Search spam report — google.com/webmasters/tools/spamreport · for SEO-spam injections.
  • Hosting provider abuse — see the WHOIS section above; most providers list an abuse@… address.
  • Registrar abuse — for the registrar of each attacker domain (listed in the WHOIS section); ICANN obliges registrars to act on documented abuse reports.
  • ICANN compliance (last resort) — icann.org/compliance/complaint if a registrar ignores a documented report.
